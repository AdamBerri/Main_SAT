Generate an SAT Math question about statistical inference that strictly adheres to SAT content standards.

CRITICAL CONTENT RESTRICTIONS:
- SAT Math tests ONLY basic statistical concepts: mean, median, mode, range, standard deviation (interpretation only), and data representation
- SAT explicitly EXCLUDES: confidence intervals, margin of error calculations, hypothesis testing, inferential statistics formulas
- Focus on conceptual understanding of samples vs. populations and interpreting study results
- NO calculations involving margin of error formulas (ME = z × σ/√n) or confidence interval construction

APPROPRIATE SAT TOPICS:
- Interpreting what a sample can/cannot tell us about a population
- Identifying bias in sampling methods or survey design
- Understanding that larger samples generally give more reliable results (conceptual only)
- Reading and interpreting statistical claims in context
- Evaluating whether conclusions from sample data are justified
- Distinguishing between correlation and causation
- Recognizing limitations of statistical studies

QUESTION STYLE REQUIREMENTS:
- Keep language concise and direct (avoid textbook verbosity)
- Use realistic, relatable contexts (surveys, studies, everyday scenarios)
- Maximum 3-4 sentence setup
- Avoid overly academic or technical phrasing
- Make the scenario feel like a standardized test item, not a statistics homework problem

SAT AUTHENTICITY — MANDATORY:
A faithful SAT inference question reads like a real College Board item. Before finalizing, verify:
- The stem presents a brief real-world context and a single clear judgment to make
- Answer choices are concise, parallel in structure, and don't repeat large chunks of the stem
- The question tests one well-defined statistical reasoning skill — it does not ask students to evaluate multiple independent issues simultaneously
- The scenario is specific enough that answer choices can be precise, not vague or hedged
- At difficulty 1-2, authentic SAT questions often ask students to identify the best estimate, the most appropriate conclusion, or the most significant limitation — NOT to solve a multi-part statistical problem
- If your question reads more like a short-answer statistics exam prompt than a multiple-choice standardized test item, revise the stem and choices to be more direct and concrete

INFERENCE VS. COMPUTATION — MANDATORY DISTINCTION:
The question MUST test statistical reasoning and inference, not arithmetic. Before finalizing, ask:
- Does answering this question require a student to evaluate the validity of a conclusion drawn from data?
- Would a student who can only compute (but not reason about inference) get it wrong?
- Is the core skill "should we trust this generalization?" rather than "what does 240/800 equal?"
If the question's primary challenge is a calculation and the inference is trivial or automatic, revise the question so the inference judgment is the central challenge. A question that reduces to: "do the proportion math, then state the scaled-up number" tests computation, not inference.

DIFFICULTY-1 QUESTION DESIGN — MANDATORY REQUIREMENTS:
Difficulty-1 inference questions have a specific failure pattern: the scenario is appropriately simple but the distractors are too weak, making the question trivially easy beyond its intended level. To avoid this:

1. DISTRACTOR QUALITY for difficulty-1 questions:
   - Even at difficulty 1, every distractor must represent a misconception a real student might hold — not a straw-man that anyone would eliminate on sight.
   - Common pitfall: writing distractors that introduce irrelevant concerns (e.g., mentioning measurement consistency when the scenario involves no measurement variation) or that contradict obvious facts in the stem.
   - Each distractor must be grounded in the scenario. Ask: "Would a student who half-understood this concept genuinely consider this choice?" If no, replace it.
   - Good difficulty-1 distractors typically represent: over-generalizing the result, under-generalizing the result, or confusing who was actually sampled — all tied to the specific scenario details.

2. AVOIDING TRIVIAL ELIMINATION at difficulty 1:
   - Do not write a distractor that is eliminable by reading comprehension alone (e.g., a claim that directly contradicts a number stated in the stem).
   - Do not write a positively-framed distractor ("The result is valid because...") in a question that asks students to identify a limitation — it requires no reasoning to eliminate.
   - Each wrong answer choice should require at least minimal inference reasoning to rule out, even at difficulty 1.

DIFFICULTY-2 QUESTION DESIGN — MANDATORY REQUIREMENTS:
Difficulty-2 inference questions have a specific failure pattern: the question concept is correct but the answer choices are weak. To avoid this:

1. ANSWER VALIDITY for difficulty-2 questions:
   - The correct answer must be unambiguously the best choice — not just "a valid concern" but clearly the most significant flaw.
   - Common pitfall: writing a correct answer that identifies a real flaw, but also writing a distractor D that is stated positively ("The result is reliable because..."). A student asked to identify a flaw will immediately eliminate any positively-framed choice — this requires zero statistical reasoning and makes the question too easy and the distractor_quality too low.
   - Never write a "positive reassurance" distractor (e.g., "The result is valid because X") in a question that asks students to identify a weakness. All distractors must be framed as explanations of a problem or flaw — they just must be the WRONG explanation.

2. DISTRACTOR DIVERSITY for difficulty-2 questions:
   - Difficulty-2 questions tend to have only 1-2 genuinely distinct flaw types available (e.g., sampling method and sample size). As a result, two distractors often end up representing the same error type in slightly different wording.
   - Before finalizing: list the error type for each of the 3 distractors. If any two share the same error type number from the taxonomy below, rewrite one to represent a different error type.
   - Specifically avoid writing two distractors that both invoke "sample size misapplication" — one saying the sample is too small and another saying a certain minimum count is required. These are the same error and will score as duplicate distractors.

3. DISTRACTOR PLAUSIBILITY for difficulty-2 questions:
   - At difficulty 2, every distractor must require at least minimal statistical reasoning to eliminate — it should not be eliminable by reading comprehension alone.
   - A distractor that directly contradicts the premise of the question (e.g., "The result is well-supported" when the question asks to identify the flaw) requires no reasoning to eliminate and is not a valid distractor.
   - A distractor that introduces a concern entirely unrelated to the scenario (e.g., "conclusions are only valid if measurement was consistent across all schools" when no multi-school comparison was made) is too contrived and will also score low. Distractors must map to a concern that is at least superficially connected to the scenario described.
   - Each distractor should represent a mistake a student who partially understands the concept might genuinely make — not a straw-man position that no one would hold.

NUMERICAL CONSISTENCY — MANDATORY CHECK:
If any specific numbers appear in the question stem or answer choices, you MUST verify them before finalizing:
1. Any number the stem claims was calculated (e.g., "a planner estimates 4,800 using this method") must actually equal what that method produces. Do the arithmetic yourself.
2. If the question is asking the student to identify a flaw in someone else's calculation, the flaw must be a reasoning error, NOT an arithmetic error the student has no way to detect without re-doing the calculation.
3. Never embed a factual arithmetic error in the premise of a question as a "trick" — this creates an answer_validity problem where the stated number conflicts with the student's own calculation.
4. For tables or multi-number setups: add up all row/column totals yourself and confirm they are internally consistent before writing the question.

ANSWER VALIDITY — MANDATORY CHECK:
Before finalizing each question, verify:
1. Substitute the correct answer back into the question context — does it fully and unambiguously answer what was asked?
2. Check each distractor — is there any interpretation where it could also be correct? If so, revise.
3. For proportional scaling questions: verify the arithmetic is correct and that the inference is valid (random sample → population)
4. For bias identification questions: ensure the bias described in the correct answer is actually present in the scenario
5. For high-difficulty questions (difficulty 4-5): the correct answer's justification must rely ONLY on information explicitly stated in the stem — not on assumed context, unstated conditions, or facts the student would need to infer before they could even evaluate the answer choices. If the correct answer requires a chain of reasoning where each step is explicitly supported by the stem, that is fine; but if a key step requires the student to assume something the stem never says, revise the stem to state it.

DIFFICULTY ACCURACY — CRITICAL AT MEDIUM LEVEL:
Medium-difficulty questions (level 3) are most prone to miscalibration. Verify:
- If a question requires only reading comprehension and recognizing one concept, it is difficulty 1-2, NOT 3
- Difficulty 3 requires the student to APPLY a statistical principle, not just recognize it
- If the question has an obvious "trick" that most prepared students would catch, it is difficulty 2
- Only rate as difficulty 3+ if a student must evaluate methodology or integrate multiple pieces of information
- A question that presents two competing conclusions and asks which is better-supported by the data is a reliable difficulty-3 structure
- A question that asks students to identify which change to a study design would most improve the validity of the inference is also reliably difficulty 3

STATISTICAL INFERENCE AUTHENTICITY:
- Questions should test understanding of inference validity, not just arithmetic scaling
- Include realistic nuance: sample representativeness, potential bias sources, appropriate generalizations
- Correct answers should address BOTH mathematical accuracy AND statistical soundness
- When a calculation yields a number, the question should ask about the validity of using that number as an inference, not just computing it
- Distractors should represent common statistical misconceptions (e.g., confusing sample size with representativeness, assuming voluntary samples are random)

DISTRACTOR QUALITY — HIGH PRIORITY:
Every distractor must reflect a specific, realistic reasoning error a student could plausibly make. Generic or vague distractors (e.g., "the sample is too small" with no further justification tied to the scenario) are not acceptable. Each distractor must be traceable to one of the following error types:

1. Confusing sample with population — treating what is true of the sample as automatically true of the entire population without considering representativeness
2. Wrong confidence interpretation — misreading the strength or scope of what a result supports (e.g., treating a suggestive trend as conclusive proof)
3. Ignoring sampling method — accepting a conclusion from a biased or non-random sample as if it were from a properly randomized one
4. Conflating correlation with causation — inferring a causal relationship from an association without accounting for confounding variables
5. Overgeneralizing scope — applying a result beyond the population actually sampled (e.g., a survey of one school's students generalized to all students nationally)
6. Sample size misapplication — believing a larger sample automatically makes an inference valid regardless of how the sample was collected
7. Misidentifying the population — concluding something about the wrong group based on who was actually surveyed

When writing distractors:
- Label each distractor internally (before writing the final JSON) with which error type it represents
- Ensure no two distractors represent the same error type — if you cannot find three distinct error types from the list above that are plausible for your scenario, revise the scenario or the question structure until you can
- Distractors should be plausible enough that a student who partially understands the concept might choose them
- Avoid distractors that are obviously absurd or that require no statistical knowledge to eliminate
- A distractor that can be eliminated without any statistical reasoning (e.g., a factually false claim, a claim that contradicts basic math a non-statistician would catch, or a positively-framed statement in a question asking to identify a flaw) is too weak — replace it with a more sophisticated misconception
- A distractor raising a concern that has no connection to the scenario's actual setup is also too weak — every distractor must be scenario-grounded
