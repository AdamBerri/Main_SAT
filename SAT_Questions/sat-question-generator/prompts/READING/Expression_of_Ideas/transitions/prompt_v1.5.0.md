Generate an SAT Writing question about transitions between ideas.

The passage should:
- Present two sentences or paragraphs where the logical relationship is IMPLIED by the content, not explicitly stated with words like "also," "but," "because," or "as a result"
- Require a specific type of transition (contrast, cause/effect, addition, or sequence)
- Create a relationship where the correct transition emerges from understanding the ideas themselves, not from matching signal words
- Avoid passages where one idea simply repeats or restates the other
- Use concrete, straightforward content without unnecessarily complex vocabulary that doesn't serve the logical reasoning task
- Even at difficulty 1/5, passages should be at least 2-3 sentences long and use academic or real-world contexts (not toy examples), matching the typical SAT passage length of 50-100+ words

CRITICAL: Distinguish linguistic complexity from task difficulty:
- Complex vocabulary or technical terminology does NOT automatically increase difficulty
- True difficulty comes from the logical reasoning required, not the sophistication of the prose
- A passage about "photosynthetic processes" with a transparent cause-effect relationship is EASIER than a simple passage requiring subtle interpretation

The question should:
- Ask which transition "most logically" connects the ideas
- Have ONE definitively correct answer that matches the ACTUAL logical relationship between the ideas
- Before finalizing, verify: Does the content REQUIRE this specific transition, or could another relationship be equally valid?
- Include distractors representing different transition types: if correct answer shows contrast (however/nevertheless), include cause/effect (therefore/consequently), addition (furthermore/additionally), and sequence (meanwhile/subsequently)

ANSWER VALIDITY - Eliminating ambiguity:
- The correct answer must be the ONLY defensible choice given the passage content
- When the passage presents a relationship that could plausibly support two transitions (e.g., "In fact" vs "Furthermore" for emphasis/addition), redesign the passage to make exactly one transition definitively correct
- Test each distractor: "Could a reasonable student argue this is correct?" If yes for any distractor, either strengthen the passage to eliminate that reading or change the distractor
- For transitions like "In fact" or "Indeed" that intensify a prior claim, ensure the passage clearly presents intensification rather than mere addition, cause-effect, or contrast
- Avoid answer choices where the distinction hinges on subtle usage conventions (e.g., "Although" vs "While" as concessive conjunctions) unless the passage structure grammatically requires one over the other
- At difficulty 3-4, pay extra attention to passages that present nuanced relationships (e.g., addition vs. escalation, contrast vs. concession). Ensure the passage content unambiguously favors one transition type by including clear directional cues in the content itself

DISTRACTOR DESIGN - Critical for difficulty calibration:
- EVERY distractor must represent a clearly DIFFERENT logical relationship from the correct answer
- Avoid pairing near-synonymous transitions as the correct answer and a distractor at difficulty levels 1-3 (e.g., do not pair "However" with "Nevertheless," or "Additionally" with "Furthermore," or "While" with "Although")
- At difficulty 4-5, near-synonymous pairs may be used as distractors ONLY if the passage structure definitively requires one over the other (e.g., "While" requires a following clause, "However" requires a new sentence)
- For 1/5 difficulty: All distractors are clearly wrong categories. Use transitions from distinctly different families (e.g., if correct is cause/effect, include contrast, addition, and sequence)
- For 2/5 difficulty: Three distractors should be obviously wrong (wrong logical direction, semantic mismatch)
- For 3/5 difficulty: At least one distractor should be superficially plausible but fail upon closer analysis; all four choices should represent DISTINCT transition categories (contrast, cause/effect, addition, sequence, concession, intensification)
- For 4/5 difficulty: Two distractors should seem defensible initially; elimination requires recognizing subtle logical distinctions
- For 5/5 difficulty: Three distractors must be genuinely plausible trap answers that exploit:
  * False synonymy (e.g., "similarly" vs. "furthermore" when ideas relate but aren't parallel)
  * Partial relationships (e.g., "therefore" when causation exists but isn't the PRIMARY connection)
  * Common misreadings (e.g., "however" when contrast is IMPLIED but not the main logical move)

Students should NOT be able to succeed through simple elimination alone at higher difficulties.

PASSAGE AUTHENTICITY:
- All passages should read like excerpts from real academic, scientific, or journalistic writing
- Even easy questions (1/5) should feature passages with a natural, professional tone -- not overly simplified or contrived
- Vary passage genres: informational, scientific, argumentative, and literary contexts
- Include contextual details (names, dates, places, statistics) that give passages verisimilitude

DIFFICULTY CALIBRATION - Test your question rigorously:

For 1/5 difficulty:
- The logical relationship is immediately obvious from everyday language
- All three distractors are clearly wrong categories (e.g., contrast distractor for a cause-effect passage)
- No ambiguity whatsoever
- Passage still uses academic language and provides real-world context

For 2/5 difficulty:
- Relationship is evident from clear context (problem -> solution = "therefore")
- Three answer choices are obviously illogical
- Single-step reasoning suffices

For 3/5 difficulty:
- Relationship requires understanding both ideas and considering which connection is strongest
- One or two distractors are superficially tempting
- Requires two-step reasoning (understand ideas + match transition type)

For 4/5 difficulty:
- Multiple transitions seem plausible initially
- Correct answer requires recognizing a subtle but definitive logical relationship
- At least two distractors exploit common misconceptions
- Requires careful comparison: "Why is X better than Y when both seem viable?"

For 5/5 difficulty:
- Passage presents ideas where the logical relationship is non-obvious
- THREE distractors must be genuinely defensible using partial evidence
- Requires multi-step analysis: understand both ideas + identify multiple possible relationships + determine which is PRIMARY + rule out attractive traps
- Students must weigh competing interpretations, not just eliminate wrong answers
- Ask: "Would strong students debate between two options?" If no, it's not 5/5

VALIDATION CHECKS before finalizing:
1. Can students eliminate 3 options easily? -> Lower the difficulty rating
2. Does complex vocabulary create difficulty, or does it come from logical ambiguity? -> Only the latter increases true difficulty
3. Is there a clear "gotcha" element where students must choose between two seemingly correct answers? -> Required for 4-5/5
4. Could the passage support a different transition type if read differently? -> Revise for clarity OR make that ambiguity intentional with trap answers
5. Re-read the passage with each answer choice inserted. Does only ONE read naturally and logically? If more than one reads well, revise the passage to eliminate the ambiguity.
6. Ensure all four answer choices belong to clearly distinct transition categories at difficulty 1-3. At difficulty 4-5, near-synonyms are permitted only when grammatical or structural cues definitively distinguish them.
7. For difficulty 3-4: After drafting the passage, explicitly identify the logical relationship type (contrast, cause/effect, addition, concession, etc.) and verify that the passage content makes this relationship unambiguous before selecting the correct answer.
