Generate an SAT Reading question about how a passage is organized or structured.

The passage should:
- Have a clear organizational pattern (compare/contrast, cause/effect, problem/solution, chronological sequence, general-to-specific, etc.)
- Use topics commonly found in SAT Reading: historical narratives, scientific explanations, social phenomena, literary commentary, biographical accounts, or policy discussions (avoid highly specialized linguistic philosophy, pure etymology, or overly technical academic discourse)
- Match structural explicitness to difficulty level:
  * Easy (1-2/5): Use explicit transition words ("however," "similarly," "as a result," "first"); state relationships directly
  * Medium (3/5): Mix some explicit markers with implicit structural cues; require moderate inference
  * Hard (4-5/5): Minimize obvious transitions; avoid phrases like "presents a paradox" or "this illustrates"; embed structure through content flow rather than explicit signposting; make readers infer relationships between sections
- Be 150-300 words with 2-4 distinct, well-developed sections
- Focus on one primary organizational pattern, but include secondary elements that support competitive alternative interpretations

The question should:
- Ask specifically about the overall structure using precise language ("How is the passage organized?" or "The passage is structured to...")
- Test understanding of rhetorical organization, not content details
- Include one clearly correct answer that accurately describes the dominant organizational pattern
- Create distractors that:
  * Are directly supported by actual textual elements (every distractor should point to something genuinely present in the passage)
  * Represent sophisticated structural misreadings, NOT content misreadings (e.g., mistaking "explanation of a phenomenon" for "solution to a problem," or "chronological sequence" for "cause-effect chain")
  * Focus on HOW information is organized, not WHAT information is present (avoid distractors based solely on counting elements or checking for topic presence)
  * Cannot be eliminated by simple presence/absence checks (if a distractor mentions "contrasting perspectives," the passage must actually contain perspectives that could plausibly be read as contrasting)

**CRITICAL — Distractor Quality at ALL Difficulty Levels:**

Even at difficulty 1-2, distractors must NOT be trivially eliminable. Follow these rules:

For difficulty 1-2 distractors:
- Every distractor must describe a structural pattern that the passage PARTIALLY exhibits — never use patterns completely absent from the text
- At least one distractor should describe a pattern that applies to ONE section of the passage but not the overall organization (e.g., the passage has a cause-effect paragraph within a larger chronological sequence)
- At least one distractor should be a closely related structural pattern that a careless reader could confuse with the correct one (e.g., "chronological sequence" vs. "cause-and-effect chain" when events are ordered but causally linked)
- NEVER include distractors that mention elements (e.g., "contrasting viewpoints," "competing theories") that are entirely absent from the passage
- Test: Cover the correct answer — would a student who skimmed the passage still find at least 2 remaining choices plausible?

For difficulty 3 distractors:
- All distractors must describe patterns genuinely present in at least one section of the passage
- The distinction between correct and incorrect should require understanding which pattern is PRIMARY vs. secondary
- Each distractor should capture a real but incomplete or subordinate structural element

Question format requirements:
- Use standard SAT phrasing and format
- Provide 4 answer choices (A-D)
- Keep answer choices concise (8-12 words average, maximum 15 words); use efficient phrasing without unnecessary clauses
- Ensure answer choices are parallel in structure and grammatically consistent
- Make distractors competitive by ensuring each describes a pattern the passage partially exhibits

Difficulty calibration:
- Rate difficulty honestly based on ALL factors combined: transition explicitness, vocabulary sophistication, inference requirements, and distractor competitiveness
- If a passage uses explicit signposting ("presents a paradox," "consider this example," "however"), it cannot be 4-5/5 difficulty regardless of vocabulary
- If distractors can be eliminated by checking simple presence/absence of topics, the question cannot be 4-5/5 difficulty
