Generate an SAT Reading question about the author's purpose.

The passage should:
- Have a clear, single primary rhetorical purpose (inform, persuade, analyze, describe, etc.)
- Show the author's intent through deliberate word choice, tone, and structure
- Be from an academic, journalistic, or literary context
- Be 6-12 sentences long with sufficient content to demonstrate purpose clearly
- Use vocabulary and style appropriate for high school students and typical SAT passages (avoid graduate-level terminology like "ideological apparatus," "hegemonic discourse," "ontological," or overly specialized academic jargon)
- Match authentic College Board tone: accessible yet substantive, clear but not simplistic

CRITICAL STRUCTURAL REQUIREMENTS FOR DIFFICULTY 3+:

Purpose Integration (MANDATORY):
- NEVER state the author's purpose explicitly in a single sentence
- Distribute evidence for purpose across 4+ sentences without concentration in opening/closing
- AVOID thesis roadmap sentences (e.g., "This framing obscures a more complex reality")
- DO NOT use transition signals that telegraph purpose: "Yet," "However," "Importantly," "What matters"
- Make purpose emerge through cumulative rhetorical patterns, not explicit declarations
- Embed purpose within content discussion rather than meta-commentary about the argument itself

For Difficulty 4+:
- Include content that genuinely supports 2-3 competing purpose interpretations
- Require synthesis across non-adjacent sentences to identify purpose
- Use rhetorical moves that could plausibly serve multiple purposes (evidence that both informs AND persuades)
- Avoid formulaic argument structures: do not follow problem→transition→solution patterns

The question should:
- Ask specifically about the author's "main purpose" or "primary purpose" in writing the passage
- Test understanding of rhetorical intent, not just topic identification
- Have one clearly correct answer that captures the author's primary goal
- Require multi-step reasoning: students must evaluate competing interpretations, not just recognize a stated thesis

ANSWER CHOICE REQUIREMENTS:

Format:
- 10-18 words each (maximum 20 words)
- Use clear, accessible vocabulary at high school reading level
- Use precise action verbs (argue, explain, criticize, advocate, analyze, demonstrate, challenge, reconcile)

Distractor Strategy (CRITICAL):
- Create at least 2 choices that are substantively defensible based on significant passage content (not just mentioned in passing)
- Design distractors that identify REAL secondary purposes or aspects actually present in the passage
- Include choices that confuse primary vs. secondary purpose (both present, but one is the organizing goal)
- Use distractors that capture accurate sub-purposes that serve the larger goal
- Avoid obviously wrong choices unrelated to passage content
- Make incorrect choices differ from correct answer by scope, emphasis, or level of purpose—not by introducing fabricated content

**CRITICAL — Distractor Quality at ALL Difficulty Levels:**

Even at difficulty 1-2, distractors must NOT be trivially eliminable:
- Every distractor must reference a real element, theme, or aspect genuinely present in the passage
- At least one distractor should describe something the passage actually DOES, but as a supporting move rather than the primary purpose (e.g., the passage provides historical context, but its purpose is to argue, not merely to inform)
- At least one distractor should capture the correct TOPIC but misidentify the rhetorical ACTION (e.g., "describe the process" when the purpose is "argue that the process is flawed")
- NEVER include distractors about topics, themes, or claims that are completely absent from the passage
- **MANDATORY for d1-d2**: Before finalizing, verify EACH distractor by locating the specific passage content it references. If a distractor cannot point to at least 2 sentences of supporting content, it is too weak — revise it to target a more substantive passage element.
- Test: Cover the correct answer — can a student who skimmed the passage still find 2 remaining choices plausible?

For Difficulty 4+:
- At least 2 distractors must require re-reading to eliminate
- Create answer choices where the distinction requires weighing what the author emphasizes most vs. what they also discuss
- Ensure competitive choices differ in rhetorical intent level: "describe X" vs. "use description of X to argue Y"

Verification Checklist Before Finalizing:
- Can I answer correctly by reading only the first and last sentences? (If yes, REVISE)
- Is there a single sentence that states the purpose explicitly? (If yes, REVISE)
- Are 3+ answer choices immediately eliminable? (If yes, strengthen distractors)
- Does the correct answer have verbatim language from a single passage sentence? (If yes, REVISE for inference requirement)
- **NEW**: For each distractor, can I identify 2+ passage sentences that make it seem plausible? (If not for any distractor, REVISE it)
