Generate an SAT Reading question that tests the student's ability to identify the central idea or main argument of a passage.

The passage should:
- Be 80-120 words for shorter passages, 150-200 for longer ones
- Use SAT-appropriate vocabulary (challenging but not graduate-level academic jargon)
- Include concrete examples, illustrations, or narrative elements alongside argumentation
- Present the central idea through development rather than direct statement
- Use varied sentence structures and paragraph breaks for authentic SAT style

**CRITICAL - Topic Complexity vs. Question Difficulty:**
- Do NOT conflate sophisticated subject matter with question difficulty
- A passage about quantum physics or historiography can still be difficulty 2-3 if the argumentative structure is straightforward
- Difficulty rating reflects how hard it is to IDENTIFY the main idea, not how advanced the topic is
- Academic vocabulary alone does not increase difficulty rating

**CRITICAL - Difficulty Calibration:**

For difficulty 2-3:
- The main idea may be stated clearly ONCE, but must be distinguished from supporting details
- Avoid placing explicit thesis statements in both opening AND closing positions
- Include at least one supporting detail that could be mistaken for the main idea

For difficulty 4-5 (APPLY STRICT STANDARDS):
- NEVER state the main idea explicitly anywhere in the passage - not in opening, closing, or middle
- NEVER use obvious structural signposting (e.g., "Yet...", "However, the key point is...", "Most importantly...", "The real significance is...", "This demonstrates that...")
- NEVER use thesis-antithesis patterns with clear pivot words
- NEVER include sentences that function as explicit roadmaps or summaries
- NEVER end with a sentence that synthesizes or concludes the main argument
- Require synthesis of 2-3 distinct but related ideas presented across the passage
- The main idea must be inferred from the relationship between these ideas, not restated
- Present ideas that seem equally weighted - avoid obvious hierarchies where one claim is clearly subordinate
- Balance concrete examples with conceptual claims so neither dominates (avoid 80% examples + 20% conclusion structures)
- If using contrasting perspectives, both must contribute essential components to the main idea (not just background vs. conclusion)
- Test: Remove any single sentence - if the passage becomes clearer or loses its main idea entirely, revise

**MANDATORY Pre-Submission Difficulty Validation:**
Before finalizing difficulty 4-5 questions, identify:
1. The sentence closest to stating the main idea → If this sentence alone captures >70% of the answer, downgrade to difficulty 3
2. Any concluding or synthesizing sentence → If present, downgrade to difficulty 3
3. Any signpost/transition that hierarchically organizes ideas → If present, downgrade to difficulty 3
4. The ratio of evidence to claim → If >75% of passage is examples supporting a clear final claim, downgrade to difficulty 3

The question should:
- Ask students to identify what the passage is "mainly about" or the author's "central claim"
- Have EXACTLY ONE defensible answer - skilled readers should reach 95%+ consensus

**Before finalizing your answer key:**
- Verify the correct answer synthesizes the full scope and emphasis of the passage
- Check that no other choice could be defensibly argued by a skilled reader
- Ensure wrong answers miss something essential or add something unsupported
- **CRITICAL**: If the passage has both methodological and substantive claims, the correct answer must capture BOTH or neither - not privilege one over the other without clear textual justification

**Eliminating Answer Ambiguity (MOST COMMON ERROR):**

When passages contain multiple legitimate perspectives or layers:
1. Ensure the correct answer encompasses the passage's PRIMARY emphasis and scope
2. If 60%+ of passage content supports interpretation X but the conclusion states interpretation Y, either:
   - Revise passage to balance content distribution
   - Make correct answer reflect the actual weight of content (favor X)
3. If both methodological claims AND substantive findings appear:
   - Determine which is FOREGROUNDED vs. used as supporting evidence
   - Correct answer should match the foregrounded element
4. Test each answer: "What percentage of passage content directly supports this interpretation?" 
   - Correct answer should reach 70%+ 
   - Distractors should fall below 50%

Distractors must:
- Include at least 2 options that accurately represent significant passage content (not minor details or contradictions)
- For difficulty 4-5: Create distractors that capture legitimate partial truths or emphasize one element of the main idea while missing the synthesized whole
- **NEW**: Distractors should capture subordinate or supporting ideas, never competing main ideas of equal textual weight
- Avoid obviously wrong choices (direct contradictions, scope violations, content not in passage)
- Not be eliminable through keyword matching or simple scope analysis alone
- Test each distractor: "Could a thoughtful reader who understood the passage defend this?" If yes for multiple options, revise passage or answers until only one is defensible

**Self-check before submitting:**
1. Structural transparency check: Can I locate a single sentence that states the main idea? (If yes, NOT difficulty 4-5)
2. Signpost check: Are there obvious transitions or roadmap sentences? (If yes, reduce difficulty rating)
3. **Answer ambiguity check (MANDATORY)**: Select your top 2 answer choices. For each, calculate what % of passage content supports it. If both exceed 60%, REVISE the passage or answers.
4. Distractor sophistication check: Do wrong answers require understanding passage content to eliminate? (Must be yes for difficulty 4-5)
5. Topic vs. difficulty check: Am I rating this high because of subject complexity rather than structural/inferential demands? (Adjust if needed)
6. **Content distribution check**: Does the passage devote 70%+ of content to supporting details/examples, then state a claim in the final sentence? (If yes, this is difficulty 3 maximum - the claim is explicitly stated)
