Generate an SAT Reading question that tests the student's ability to identify the central idea or main argument of a passage.

The passage should:
- Be 80-120 words for shorter passages, 150-200 for longer ones
- Present a central idea that emerges through cumulative evidence across the ENTIRE passage
- Distribute key evidence across multiple sentences—never concentrate the main idea in any single sentence, especially the conclusion
- Avoid thesis signaling phrases like "The fundamental problem is...", "The key point is...", "Most importantly...", "In essence...", "Ultimately..."
- Use vocabulary appropriate for advanced high school students; avoid graduate-level specialized terminology
- Come from academic or literary contexts (science, history, social studies, literature)
- Structure passages to resist simple summarization: use non-linear organization, embed main points within examples, or present evidence before interpretation

The question should:
- Ask students to identify what the passage is "mainly about" or the author's "central claim"
- Have one answer that synthesizes the passage's main idea at the appropriate level of abstraction
- Include distractors that create genuine decision points for capable students:
  * **Competitive scope alternatives**: Ideas that are legitimately important but one level too specific or too broad (not obviously so)
  * **Partial synthesis traps**: Answers that correctly combine 2 of 3 key elements but miss a crucial nuance
  * **Emphasis misreadings**: Ideas that are present and discussed but are contextual setup rather than the main point
  * **Avoid obvious eliminators**: No absolute language ("only", "never"), extreme claims, clear scope mismatches, or trivial details

Calibrate difficulty realistically:
- Difficulty comes from synthesis requirements and passage structure, NOT vocabulary
- Level 3/5: Main idea emerges from 2-3 clearly related points; one distractor targets the most prominent detail
- Level 4/5: Main idea requires connecting ideas from different parts of the passage that aren't explicitly linked; at least two distractors represent defensible partial readings
- Level 5/5: Main idea requires inferring an unstated through-line connecting 3+ dispersed elements; multiple answer choices should seem defensible on first reading

Critical constraints:
- No single sentence should contain the complete main idea in paraphrasable form
- The correct answer should require mental synthesis that goes beyond restating any passage sentence
- At least two distractors must be tempting to students who understood the passage but weighted evidence differently
- For level 4-5: The correct answer should capture something more abstract or integrative than any individual passage claim
