Generate an SAT Reading question that tests the student's ability to identify the central idea or main argument of a passage through genuine synthesis.

The passage should:
- Be 80-120 words for shorter passages, 150-200 for longer ones
- Present a central idea that emerges through cumulative evidence across the ENTIRE passage
- Distribute key evidence across multiple sentences using VARIED structure—avoid parallel examples or repetitive patterns that make the main idea obvious
- Never include a topic sentence or thesis statement that could be matched to the correct answer
- Avoid thesis signaling phrases like "The fundamental problem is...", "The key point is...", "Most importantly...", "In essence...", "Ultimately...", "This shows...", "Therefore..."
- Use vocabulary appropriate for advanced high school students; avoid graduate-level specialized terminology
- Come from academic or literary contexts (science, history, social studies, literature)
- Structure passages to resist simple summarization: embed main points within examples, present evidence before interpretation, or use non-linear organization where different paragraphs/sections address different aspects

The question should:
- Ask students to identify what the passage is "mainly about" or the author's "central claim"
- Have one answer that synthesizes the passage's main idea at the appropriate level of abstraction
- Include distractors that create genuine decision points by being defensible misreadings:
  * **Competitive scope alternatives**: Ideas one level too specific or too broad, but not obviously so—these should capture real passage content
  * **Partial synthesis traps**: Answers that correctly combine 2 of 3 key elements, or capture the passage's most emphasized point without the underlying through-line
  * **Emphasis misreadings**: Ideas that receive substantial discussion but serve as context/setup rather than the synthesized main point
  * **Avoid obvious eliminators**: No extreme language, trivial details mentioned once, or clear scope mismatches

Calibrate difficulty realistically - be honest about actual cognitive demand:
- Level 2/5: Main idea stated in one sentence that can be paraphrased; one distractor targets most prominent detail
- Level 3/5: Main idea emerges from combining 2-3 related points that appear in proximity; most distractors are clearly too narrow/broad
- Level 4/5: Main idea requires connecting ideas from different parts that aren't explicitly linked; at least two distractors represent plausible partial readings that capture 60-70% of the passage's focus
- Level 5/5: Main idea requires inferring an unstated through-line connecting 3+ dispersed elements; three distractors should seem defensible, with at least one capturing the passage's most emphasized content while missing the synthesized core

Critical quality checks before finalizing:
1. Can any single sentence be paraphrased to match the correct answer? If yes, revise passage
2. Do distractors represent genuinely different interpretive lenses, not just scope errors? If no, strengthen distractors
3. Does the passage use varied rhetorical moves (not just parallel examples)? If no, diversify structure
4. Would a skilled student who understood everything need to make a difficult judgment call between 2-3 answers? If no, improve answer architecture
5. Is the stated difficulty rating honest about actual cognitive demand? If main idea is explicit or distractors are weak, lower the rating

For level 4-5 questions specifically:
- The correct answer must be MORE abstract/integrative than any individual passage claim
- At least two distractors must accurately reflect substantial passage content
- The passage should require students to distinguish between "most discussed" and "synthesized main point"
